What is a SIP and why every salaried person should start one

By Advit Capital Advisors June 10, 2025 5 min read

If you've ever felt that investing is complicated, risky, or only for the wealthy — this post is for you. A Systematic Investment Plan, or SIP, is one of the simplest and most powerful ways to build wealth over time. And the best part? You can start with just ₹500 a month.

What exactly is a SIP?

A SIP is a method of investing in mutual funds where you invest a fixed amount at regular intervals — usually monthly — instead of putting in a large lump sum at once. Think of it like a recurring deposit, except your money is invested in the market and has the potential to grow significantly over the long term.

💡 Think of it this way: A SIP is not a type of mutual fund — it's a method of investing in mutual funds. You choose the fund; the SIP is simply your commitment to invest regularly.

How does a SIP work?

When you start a SIP, a fixed amount is automatically debited from your bank account every month and invested in your chosen mutual fund. You receive units of the fund at the prevailing NAV (Net Asset Value — the price per unit on that day).

Because you invest at different NAVs each month, you automatically benefit from Rupee Cost Averaging: you buy more units when prices are low, and fewer when prices are high. This reduces the impact of market volatility on your overall portfolio.

The power of compounding

The biggest advantage of a SIP is time. The longer you stay invested, the harder compounding works for you. Here's a simple example:

Why SIPs are perfect for salaried individuals

  • Automatic & disciplined: The amount debits automatically — no willpower needed.
  • Flexible: You can pause, increase, or stop anytime without penalty.
  • Low entry barrier: Start with as little as ₹500/month.
  • No need to time the market: Regular investing smooths out market ups and downs.

Common mistakes to avoid

Many first-time investors make the mistake of stopping their SIP when markets fall. This is actually the worst time to stop — falling markets mean you're buying units at a lower price, which benefits you when markets recover.

📌 Golden rule: Stay invested through market downturns. Short-term noise should not interrupt long-term wealth creation.
