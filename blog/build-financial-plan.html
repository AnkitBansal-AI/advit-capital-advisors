How to Build a Personal Financial Plan from Scratch

By Advit Capital Advisors June 19, 2025 10 min read

Most people think a financial plan is a stack of spreadsheets, a portfolio of SIPs, or a document their CA hands them at the end of tax season. It is none of these things. A financial plan is simply a clear, honest map — from where you are right now, financially, to where you want to be at specific points in your life. Everything else — the funds, the insurance, the investments — is just the vehicle. The map comes first.

This post walks you through building that map, step by step, from nothing. No finance degree required. No large corpus needed. Just honest answers to seven questions about your own life and money.

📋 How to use this article: Read it once end-to-end, then come back and work through each step with your own numbers. If you're doing this as a couple, do it together — financial plans work best when both partners have built them jointly and understand every number in them.

The 7 steps to a complete personal financial plan

1

Know your exact financial position today

You cannot plan a journey without knowing your starting point. Before any investment decision, any SIP amount, any goal — you need a clear, honest picture of your current financial reality. This means building a simple net worth statement.

Net worth = Everything you own (assets) minus everything you owe (liabilities). Most people have never calculated this number — and many are surprised to find it's more positive, or more negative, than they assumed.

Update this snapshot every year. Watching your net worth grow annually is one of the most motivating things in personal finance — and the only honest measure of whether your financial plan is actually working.

2

Understand your monthly cash flow

Net worth tells you where you stand. Cash flow tells you how fast you're moving. Your monthly cash flow is your income minus your expenses — and the gap between them is what you have available to invest and save.

The simplest framework for thinking about cash flow is the 50/30/20 rule: allocate 50% to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings and investments. While the exact percentages will vary for your situation, the structure is a useful starting point.

If you're currently saving less than 20%, don't panic — start where you are and increase by 1–2% each time you get a raise. The key is that savings must be automatic: SIPs and recurring transfers should happen on salary day, before you spend anything. 3

Build your emergency fund first

Before you invest a single rupee in equity, you need a financial buffer that protects your investments from being disrupted by life. This is your emergency fund — liquid money that covers 3 to 6 months of essential expenses, held in a savings account or liquid mutual fund where you can access it within 24 hours.

Without an emergency fund, the first medical bill or job disruption forces you to break your SIPs, redeem your investments at exactly the wrong time, or go into high-interest debt. The emergency fund is not an investment — it is insurance for your investment plan.

4

Map your goals with precision

Vague goals produce vague plans. "I want to retire comfortably" is not a goal. "I want a retirement corpus of ₹3 crore by age 58 to generate a monthly income of ₹80,000 in today's money" is a goal — because it can be worked backwards into a specific monthly SIP amount, invested in specific fund categories, reviewed at specific intervals.

For each goal, you need four things: what it is, when you need it, how much it costs in today's money, and how much it will cost accounting for inflation.

Each goal then determines which type of fund you use. Short-term goals (under 3 years) → debt funds or liquid funds. Medium-term goals (3–7 years) → balanced / hybrid funds. Long-term goals (7+ years) → equity mutual funds, with decreasing risk as you approach the goal date.

5

Protect what you're building — insurance

Insurance is the foundation that keeps your financial plan from collapsing under the weight of life's unexpected events. No investment plan survives an uninsured medical emergency or the untimely death of the primary earner without insurance in place. Get this right before you worry about which funds to pick.

Two covers are non-negotiable for any earning adult with dependents:

🛡️

Term Life Insurance

Pure life cover that pays a lump sum to your family if you pass away during the policy term. No investment component — just maximum cover at minimum cost. Far superior to ULIPs or endowment plans for protection purposes.

Cover = 10–15× annual income
🏥

Health Insurance

Covers hospitalisation and major medical expenses. A single serious illness without adequate health cover can wipe out years of investment savings. Corporate covers provided by employers are often insufficient — supplement with a personal family floater.

Minimum ₹10–15 lakh cover per family
⚠️ What insurance is NOT: Insurance is not an investment. ULIPs, money-back policies, and endowment plans are insurance products sold with an investment component — they typically do both jobs poorly and at high cost. Keep your protection (term insurance) and investment (mutual funds) completely separate for maximum efficiency.
6

Build and deploy your investment portfolio

Now — and only now, once steps 1–5 are in place — you are ready to invest. With your goals mapped, your timeline known, and your risk profile understood, you can build a portfolio where every rupee has a specific purpose.

The core principle: match the asset class to the time horizon.

  • 0–3 years: Liquid funds, short-duration debt funds, FDs. Capital protection is the priority. Equity is too volatile for this window.
  • 3–7 years: Balanced advantage funds, aggressive hybrid funds, or a mix of large cap equity and debt. Moderate growth with managed risk.
  • 7+ years: Equity mutual funds — large cap, flexi cap, mid cap, small cap — based on your risk profile. This is where compounding does its best work.

Start each goal with its own SIP, clearly labelled in your records. When you invest, tag each investment to the goal it belongs to — retirement SIP, education SIP, home fund SIP. This prevents the common mistake of redeeming your 20-year retirement SIP early to fund a 5-year goal.

7

Review, rebalance, and evolve your plan

A financial plan is not a one-time document. It is a living structure that must be reviewed as your life, income, and goals evolve. Markets change. Children grow up. Salaries increase. Jobs change. The plan that was right for you at 30 needs meaningful updates at 35, 40, and beyond.

Build a simple review calendar into your year:

Monthly

Cash flow check

Are SIPs running? Is savings rate maintained? Any new debt?

Quarterly

Portfolio glance

Review fund performance vs benchmark. No action unless significantly off-track.

Annually

Full plan review

Update net worth, reassess goals, rebalance asset allocation, tax planning.

On life events

Plan revision

New job, marriage, child, home purchase — each triggers a fresh plan update.

The annual review is the most important. Block a specific date each year — perhaps your birthday or the start of the financial year — and treat it like a fixed appointment. Review your net worth, compare it to last year, check whether your SIP amounts still match your goal targets, and discuss any life changes with your advisor.

Your complete financial plan — a one-page summary

Once you've worked through all seven steps, your financial plan can be summarised on a single page. Here's what that page contains:

Your personal financial plan checklist

📊

Net worth statement

All assets and liabilities listed, net worth calculated, updated annually.

💰

Monthly budget with savings rate

Income, expenses categorised, savings rate tracked, SIPs auto-deducted on salary day.

🔒

Emergency fund fully funded

6 months of essential expenses in a liquid, accessible account.

🎯

Goals mapped with timelines and amounts

Each goal has a name, a date, a cost (inflation-adjusted), and an assigned SIP.

🛡️

Term and health insurance in place

Adequate cover for life (10–15× income) and health (₹10L+ family floater).

📈

Investment portfolio deployed

Each SIP tagged to a goal, asset class matched to time horizon, portfolio reviewed annually.

📅

Annual review scheduled

Fixed date in the calendar every year to update the plan with new numbers and life changes.

✅ The most important thing to remember: A perfect financial plan that never gets implemented is worth nothing. An imperfect plan that is actually followed is worth everything. Start with what you have, where you are, with whatever amount you can commit to today. Build the habit first. Refine the plan as your knowledge and income grow. The first step — just beginning — is the one that matters most.
