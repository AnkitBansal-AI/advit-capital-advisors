5 Questions to Ask Before Choosing a Financial Advisor

By Advit Capital Advisors June 15, 2025 7 min read

Choosing a financial advisor is one of the most important financial decisions you'll make — yet most people spend less time on it than they do choosing a smartphone. They go with a friend's recommendation, a bank's in-house advisor, or whoever happens to reach out at the right moment.

The result? Portfolios full of the wrong products, misaligned risk, and advisors who disappear after the first transaction. The relationship that should have lasted decades fizzles in months.

Before you trust anyone with your financial future, ask these five questions. Know what a great answer sounds like — and know exactly what should make you walk away.

📋 How to use this article: Print this or keep it handy during your first meeting with any advisor. The questions are designed to be asked naturally, in conversation. A good advisor will welcome them. A poor one will be evasive or defensive — and that tells you everything you need to know.
1

"How do you earn from our relationship?"

The money question — ask it first, without apology

This is the most important question, and the one most investors are too polite to ask. How an advisor is compensated directly shapes what they recommend — whether they admit it or not. There are two main models in India:

Commission-based (distributor model): The advisor earns a commission from the AMC when you invest in a fund through them. This is the most common model in India. It's not inherently bad — but you should know which funds carry higher commissions and whether that influences recommendations.

Fee-only (RIA model): The advisor charges you directly — a flat fee, hourly rate, or percentage of AUM — and earns nothing from the products they recommend. Fewer advisors operate this way in India, but it eliminates commission-based bias entirely.

✅ Good answer
  • "I earn a distributor commission from the AMC — here's roughly what that looks like for different fund categories."
  • "I charge a flat advisory fee of ₹X per year, and I don't earn anything from the products I recommend."
  • Clear, specific, and delivered without defensiveness
🚩 Red flag
  • "Don't worry about that — we make sure clients get the best returns."
  • Vague, deflective, or irritated by the question
  • Claims to earn "nothing" from your investments without explaining how they're actually compensated
⚠️ Watch out for

Advisors who push insurance-linked investment products (ULIPs, endowment plans) aggressively — these typically carry the highest commissions in the industry and are rarely the best investment option for most investors.

2

"What is your investment philosophy?"

You're looking for conviction — not jargon

Every advisor has a philosophy — a set of core beliefs about how wealth is built and what their role is in that process. Some believe in active stock picking; others believe in passive, low-cost index funds; others take a goal-based, behavioural coaching approach. None of these is inherently wrong — but you need to know what yours is, and whether it aligns with the advisor's.

What you're listening for is clarity and consistency. An advisor who gives you a different answer depending on what you seem to want to hear doesn't actually have a philosophy — they have a sales approach.

✅ Good answer
  • Has a clear, consistent view: "I believe in long-term, goal-mapped investing with minimal portfolio churn."
  • Can explain the reasoning behind their approach without referencing recent market performance
  • Philosophy doesn't change depending on what's currently "hot"
  • Focuses on your behaviour as much as fund selection
🚩 Red flag
  • Focuses entirely on "beating the market" or "best returns"
  • Shifts the conversation immediately to specific fund names or recent performance numbers
  • Philosophy is described vaguely: "We look at all options and pick the best for you"
3

"How do you handle a client whose portfolio is down 30%?"

The most revealing question of all

This question tells you more about an advisor's real value than any other. Markets will fall — significantly, at some point during your investment journey. It's not a question of if, but when. The real question is: what does your advisor do when that happens?

Bad advisors disappear during market downturns. Or worse, they panic alongside their clients and suggest switching funds or moving to "safer" options at exactly the wrong moment. Good advisors have thought about this carefully, have a plan, and make proactive contact when markets are stressed.

✅ Good answer
  • "I reach out to clients proactively during significant market falls — before they call me in panic."
  • "We discuss downside scenarios before we start investing, so clients are mentally prepared when it happens."
  • "A 30% fall is temporary; selling turns it permanent. My job is to help clients see through the noise."
  • Has a documented communication plan for market events
🚩 Red flag
  • "We'd move to safer funds / FDs / gold until things stabilise."
  • Vague: "We'd review the portfolio and take appropriate action."
  • No clear process — just reacts based on client pressure
  • Has never actually experienced managing clients through a major crash
⚠️ Why this matters so much

The biggest wealth-destroying events in investing happen during market downturns — not because of bad fund selection, but because of bad decisions under stress. An advisor who can keep you invested during a crash is worth more than any fund recommendation they'll ever make.

4

"How often will we review my portfolio, and who initiates it?"

Great advisors are proactive, not reactive

Many advisors are excellent at the initial portfolio setup — and then essentially disappear. Clients have to chase them for reviews, their calls aren't returned promptly, and the relationship becomes transactional. This is a widespread problem in the industry, and it's one of the main reasons investors feel underserved despite "having an advisor."

A genuine advisory relationship involves scheduled, regular reviews — not just when you remember to ask. Your life changes. Markets evolve. Your portfolio should respond to both.

✅ Good answer
  • "We do a formal portfolio review every 6 months — I schedule and initiate it."
  • "If something significant changes in the market or in a fund you hold, I reach out — I don't wait for you to call."
  • "We also do unscheduled check-ins when major life events happen — job change, new child, large expense coming up."
🚩 Red flag
  • "You can call anytime if you have questions." (No proactive review process)
  • "We review annually" — with no clarity on who schedules it or what it covers
  • Only seems interested in adding new investments, not reviewing existing ones
5

"Are you AMFI registered, and can I verify your credentials?"

Never skip this — no matter how trustworthy they seem

In India, anyone distributing mutual funds must be registered with AMFI (Association of Mutual Funds in India) and hold a valid ARN (AMFI Registration Number). This is non-negotiable. It's the baseline credential that ensures the person you're dealing with has passed the NISM exam, follows SEBI guidelines, and is accountable to a regulatory framework.

Similarly, if someone is providing investment advice (not just distributing funds), they should ideally be a SEBI Registered Investment Advisor (RIA) — a more stringent designation that comes with a fiduciary duty toward clients.

✅ Good answer
  • Provides their ARN number immediately and without hesitation
  • Explains the difference between an MFD (distributor) and RIA (advisor) and tells you which they are
  • Welcomes you to verify their registration on the AMFI or SEBI website
  • Has no history of SEBI or AMFI disciplinary action
🚩 Red flag
  • Hesitant, vague, or dismissive about credentials
  • "I've been in this field for 20 years" — without providing verifiable registration details
  • Makes promises about guaranteed returns (illegal under SEBI regulations)
  • Operates informally with no proper documentation or written agreements
⚠️ How to verify

You can verify any mutual fund distributor's ARN on the AMFI website at amfiindia.com. For SEBI Registered Investment Advisors, check sebi.gov.in. Takes two minutes and should always be done before transferring any money.

Beyond the questions: what a good relationship feels like

The right advisor doesn't just answer these five questions well. Over time, a good advisory relationship has a particular quality to it — one that's hard to quantify but easy to recognise.

You understand every investment you hold

If you can't explain in plain language why each fund is in your portfolio, your advisor hasn't done their job. Informed investors make better decisions.

You feel calmer about money, not anxious

A good advisor reduces your financial anxiety — not by giving rosy projections, but by helping you feel genuinely prepared for both good and bad market environments.

They say "no" when it's the right answer

An advisor who agrees with every idea you bring is not serving your interests. The best advisors push back thoughtfully when a decision isn't in your long-term interest.

You trust them enough to share the full picture

Good advice requires complete information — income, debts, other investments, family situation. If you're hiding things from your advisor, the relationship isn't working.

Your pre-meeting checklist

Before your first meeting with any advisor, prepare the following — it will save time and immediately signal that you're a serious, organised client (which advisors notice and appreciate):

✓

A rough sense of your monthly income and expenses — you don't need exact numbers, just order of magnitude

✓

A list of your current investments, if any — SIPs, FDs, PPF, EPF, insurance policies, real estate

✓

Your top 2–3 financial goals, with rough timelines ("child's college in 14 years", "retire by 55")

✓

Any large upcoming expenses in the next 3 years — home purchase, wedding, home loan repayment

✓

The five questions from this article, either memorised or on your phone — ask all of them
