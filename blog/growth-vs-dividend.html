Growth vs Dividend option: what should you choose?

By Advit Capital Advisors June 11, 2025 6 min read

When you invest in a mutual fund, one of the first choices you'll be asked to make is: Growth or Dividend? For most new investors, this feels like a coin flip. It isn't. The two options behave very differently, and choosing the wrong one can quietly cost you — especially on the tax front.

Let's break it down properly, without the jargon.

First — what do these options actually mean?

Both options invest in the exact same underlying portfolio of stocks or bonds. The fund manager doesn't change. The returns the fund earns are identical. What differs is what happens to the profits the fund makes.

Option 1

📈 Growth

  • All profits stay invested inside the fund
  • Your NAV (unit price) keeps rising over time
  • No payouts — money compounds silently
  • You only realise gains when you redeem
  • Tax is paid only at the time of redemption
Option 2

💰 Dividend (IDCW)

  • Fund periodically pays out a portion of profits
  • Your NAV drops by the payout amount after each dividend
  • Payouts land in your bank account
  • Not guaranteed — declared at the fund's discretion
  • Tax is paid every time a dividend is received
📋 A name change you should know: SEBI renamed the "Dividend" option to IDCW — Income Distribution cum Capital Withdrawal in 2021. It's the same thing. You'll see both names used on fund platforms. Don't let it confuse you.

The tax reality — and why it matters more than most realise

This is where the choice gets serious. The tax treatment of the two options is fundamentally different, and for most investors, it tilts the decision firmly in one direction.

Growth option — tax on redemption

You pay capital gains tax only when you sell your units. For equity funds, the rates are:

  • Short-term capital gains (held under 1 year): 20%
  • Long-term capital gains (held over 1 year): 12.5% on gains above ₹1.25 lakh per year

If you stay invested for the long term and don't redeem, you pay zero tax in the interim. Your money compounds uninterrupted.

Dividend (IDCW) option — tax every time

Every dividend you receive is added to your income and taxed at your income tax slab rate. If you're in the 30% bracket, you lose 30% of every payout to tax — every single time a dividend is declared.

⚠️ The dividend illusion: Many investors think dividends are "free money" or "bonus returns." They're not. When a fund pays ₹2 as dividend, the NAV drops by exactly ₹2. You haven't gained anything — you've simply moved money from your investment account to your bank account, and paid tax on it along the way.

A side-by-side comparison

Factor Growth Option Dividend (IDCW) Option
What happens to profits Reinvested, NAV grows Paid out periodically
Compounding Full, uninterrupted Reduced — profits leave the fund
Tax trigger Only at redemption Every time dividend is paid
Tax rate (equity funds) 10–20% on gains As per your income slab (up to 30%)
Income in hand None until you sell Regular payouts
Dividends guaranteed? N/A No — at fund's discretion
Best for Long-term wealth creation Retirees needing cash flow

So when does the dividend option actually make sense?

There are legitimate cases where IDCW is the better choice:

  • Retirees or those with no regular income who need periodic cash flow from their investments to cover living expenses — and whose total income is below the taxable threshold (₹3–7 lakh depending on age), meaning dividends attract little or no tax.
  • Investors in the nil or low tax bracket for whom the tax disadvantage is minimal, and having money in-hand suits their financial behaviour better.
  • Short-horizon debt fund investors — the tax math can sometimes work differently here; worth a conversation with your advisor.
✅ Exception worth noting: If you are in the 0% tax bracket (income below ₹3 lakh), dividends from equity funds are effectively tax-free for you. In this specific case, IDCW becomes more interesting — though the compounding argument still favours Growth for very long horizons.

The wealth creation maths — Growth wins over time

Here's a simplified illustration of how ₹10 lakh invested in the same equity fund plays out over 15 years, assuming 12% annualised returns and a 30% tax slab for the investor:

The gap widens the longer you stay invested. Compounding is brutally simple: every rupee taxed away today is a rupee that can't compound for 15 more years.

The Advit Capital View

For most investors under 55: choose Growth.

Unless you genuinely need periodic income from your investments right now, the Growth option is almost always the smarter choice. It lets your money compound without interruption, defers your tax liability to the point of redemption, and gives you control over when you realise gains. The dividend option sounds appealing — regular payouts feel rewarding — but you're essentially withdrawing your own money, paying tax on it, and losing years of compounding in the process.

One last thing — SWP is better than dividend for income needs

If you want regular income from your mutual fund investment — say, in retirement — there's a more tax-efficient way to do it than the dividend option: a Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP).

With an SWP, you instruct the fund to redeem a fixed amount of units every month and credit it to your bank account. Because only the gains portion of each redemption is taxed (not the full amount, unlike dividends), your effective tax outgo is significantly lower. And your remaining corpus stays invested and compounding.

We'll cover SWPs in detail in a future post. But the short version: SWP is the dividend option done right.
