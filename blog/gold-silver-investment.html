Gold & Silver as Investments: What Every Indian Investor Should Know

By Advit Capital Advisors June 16, 2025 9 min read

Gold runs deep in the Indian psyche. We wear it at weddings, gift it at births, and store it in lockers as a financial safety net. Silver isn't far behind — it fills our puja rooms and jewellery boxes. But there's a crucial difference between owning precious metals for cultural reasons and investing in them strategically for financial returns.

This post cuts through the emotion and answers the questions that actually matter: Are gold and silver good investments? How should you hold them? And how much — if any — belongs in your portfolio?

Understanding gold and silver as asset classes

Before deciding whether to invest, you need to understand what kind of asset you're actually dealing with. Gold and silver are fundamentally different from equities or debt — and that difference shapes everything about how you should use them.

🥇

Gold

The original safe-haven asset

Gold has been a store of value for over 5,000 years. It doesn't rust, corrode, or decay. Central banks hold it. Governments stockpile it. In times of crisis — war, inflation, currency collapse — gold tends to hold its value when everything else falls.

In India specifically, gold also has a currency dimension: since gold is priced in USD globally, a weakening rupee tends to push domestic gold prices higher, providing a natural hedge against currency depreciation.

Primary roleWealth preservation
Historical return (India, 20yr)~10–11% p.a.
VolatilityModerate
Correlation with equityLow to negative
🥈

Silver

Industrial metal meets investment asset

Silver sits in an unusual position — it's simultaneously an investment asset and a heavily used industrial commodity. It goes into solar panels, electric vehicles, electronics, and medical equipment. This dual nature means silver is more volatile than gold and more sensitive to economic cycles.

When economies boom, silver prices often rise faster than gold (industrial demand surges). When recessions hit, silver can fall harder. It offers higher return potential than gold — but with noticeably more risk.

Primary roleGrowth + hedge
Historical return (India, 20yr)~11–13% p.a.
VolatilityHigh
Correlation with equityLow to moderate
💡 The key insight: Neither gold nor silver generates income. They pay no dividends, no interest, no rent. Their return comes entirely from price appreciation. This makes them fundamentally different from equities (which grow through business earnings) and debt (which generates interest). They are stores of value — not engines of wealth creation.

Why gold and silver belong in a portfolio — even if returns aren't the point

If gold and silver don't generate income and have historically returned less than well-chosen equity mutual funds, why should any investor hold them? The answer lies in a concept called correlation.

Gold has a low — and often negative — correlation with equity markets. This means when stock markets crash badly, gold often goes up, or at least doesn't fall as much. During the 2008 global financial crisis, Indian equities fell roughly 55%. Gold in India rose about 25% in the same period. The portfolio that held 15–20% in gold fell far less, recovered faster, and crucially — kept investors from panic-selling their equities at the bottom.

This is the real job of precious metals in a portfolio: not to deliver the highest returns, but to reduce overall portfolio volatility and keep you stable during equity storms.

🛡️

Crisis Hedge

Performs well when equities crash — reducing overall portfolio drawdown during market panics.

📉

Inflation Hedge

Gold historically preserves purchasing power over long periods when inflation erodes the value of cash and bonds.

💱

Rupee Hedge

Since gold is USD-priced, a falling rupee pushes domestic gold prices up — protecting Indian investors from currency risk.

The five ways to invest in gold and silver in India

This is where most investors go wrong. How you hold precious metals matters enormously — for costs, safety, returns, and tax efficiency.

🪙

Physical Gold / Silver (Jewellery, coins, bars)

The traditional way. Emotionally satisfying, but financially inefficient. Making charges on jewellery (10–25%) are a dead cost you never recover. Storage risk. No liquidity on weekends. You sell at a discount to spot price.

✓ Cultural value ✗ High making charges ✗ Storage risk ✗ Poor liquidity
📜

Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs)

Issued by the RBI, SGBs are the gold investment for wealth-builders. You earn 2.5% p.a. interest on top of gold price appreciation. If held to maturity (8 years), capital gains are completely tax-free. No storage risk whatsoever. The single best way to invest in gold in India.

✓ 2.5% interest ✓ Tax-free at maturity ✓ Government backed ✗ 8-year lock-in
📊

Gold / Silver ETFs

Exchange-traded funds that track gold or silver prices, held in your demat account. Extremely liquid — you can buy or sell any time during market hours. Low expense ratios (0.3–0.8%). No storage concerns. Taxed as debt funds (if held under 3 years) or at 20% with indexation (over 3 years). Best for investors who want flexibility and don't want the 8-year lock-in of SGBs.

✓ High liquidity ✓ Low cost ~ Taxable gains
🏦

Gold / Silver Mutual Funds

Fund-of-funds that invest in gold ETFs. Unlike ETFs, you don't need a demat account — making them accessible via SIP through your regular mutual fund platform. Slightly higher expense ratio than direct ETFs, but ideal for investors who want to do a monthly SIP in gold without a trading account.

✓ SIP available ✓ No demat needed ~ Slightly higher cost
📱

Digital Gold (Paytm, PhonePe, etc.)

Convenient but carries counterparty risk — you're trusting a private company to hold physical gold on your behalf. Less regulatory oversight than ETFs or SGBs. Better than buying jewellery impulsively, but not the cleanest investment structure for serious wealth-building. Use SGBs or ETFs instead.

~ Easy to start ✗ Counterparty risk ✗ Less regulated

Common myths about gold investment — debunked

🚫 "Gold always goes up — it's the safest investment"
Gold can and does fall — significantly. Between 2012 and 2015, gold prices in India fell over 20%. Between 2020 and 2022, they were largely flat. Gold is a long-term store of value, not a guaranteed annual compounder. It is not a substitute for equity in a wealth-creation portfolio.
🚫 "Buying gold jewellery is an investment"
Jewellery is a consumption product with an embedded cost (making charges of 10–25%) that you never recover. When you sell, you get the melt value — not the retail price. Jewellery is a perfectly fine cultural and personal choice, but it is not an efficient financial investment. For investment purposes, use SGBs or ETFs.
🚫 "Silver is just cheap gold — it'll catch up eventually"
Silver has a different demand profile than gold — heavily industrial, more volatile, and driven by different macro forces. It doesn't "catch up" to gold in any consistent way. It can dramatically outperform gold in boom cycles and dramatically underperform during recessions. Treat it as a separate, higher-risk asset class — not a budget version of gold.
🚫 "I should increase gold allocation when prices are rising"
This is chasing performance — the same mistake investors make with equity funds. The right time to add gold to your portfolio is when your asset allocation calls for it, not when gold is making headlines for its price surge. Buying at peaks and selling during fear is the surest way to underperform.
✅ The smart approach in one paragraph: For most Indian investors, gold deserves a place in a well-diversified portfolio — roughly 8–12% — held via Sovereign Gold Bonds for long-term holdings (tax-free at maturity) or Gold ETFs for flexibility. Silver can complement gold at a smaller 3–5% allocation via a Silver ETF or fund. Neither should be held primarily as jewellery for investment purposes. And neither should replace equity mutual funds as the primary engine of long-term wealth creation.
